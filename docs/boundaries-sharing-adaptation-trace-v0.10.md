# SeedPEA Boundaries, Sharing, Adaptation, and Trace Extension

Community Branch Extension v0.10  
Companion file to SeedPEA Community Branch Draft v0.10

## Extension Status

This extension supports the SeedPEA Community Branch v0.10 core document. It expands the public boundaries around use, adaptation, responsibility, surveillance, legal posture, stewardship, provenance, and trace.

It is not a replacement for the core operator interface. It exists so the core can stay readable while deeper boundary material remains available.

This extension is public-facing. It does not release private implementation internals, private weights, thresholds, handler logic, routing internals, unpublished code, trade secrets, business materials, private correspondence, or private working notes.

Short rule:

> Seed useful beginnings. Keep ethical boundaries active. Preserve only what can responsibly carry forward.

## 1. Purpose of This Extension

SeedPEA is meant to help people and systems offer cleaner assistance. That does not mean every use of SeedPEA is automatically clean. A framework can be useful and still be misused. A branch can use SeedPEA language while weakening SeedPEA's ethical core. An institution can describe a system as "SeedPEA-inspired" while using it to make authority inflation, monitoring, or disclosure harder to see.

This extension clarifies what SeedPEA can help with, what it cannot authorize, who remains responsible when it is used, how adaptations should be labeled, how stewardship and governance claims should be handled, how trace should be weighed against account/name/platform/institutional claims, and how surveillance, monitoring, investigation, disclosure, and enforcement uses must remain bounded.

SeedPEA should promote honesty and growth, not surveillance and control.

## 2. What SeedPEA Can Do

SeedPEA can be used as a public-facing interaction guide. It can help a person, group, model, product, classroom, research project, community, or institution design assistance that is more useful, bounded, transparent, and repairable.

SeedPEA can help with prompt design, response review, AI interaction norms, public release discipline, privacy-aware drafting, claim-strength checking, child/dependent protection, companion-interface boundaries, REWASH, adaptation labeling, governance questions, provenance questions, and reducing authority inflation.

SeedPEA can provide a route, vocabulary, checklist, prompt, repair method, or community branch structure. It can make responsibility easier to see. It cannot remove responsibility.

## 3. What SeedPEA Cannot Do

SeedPEA cannot create authority that was not already present. It cannot turn a preference into law, suspicion into proof, usefulness into certification, a branch into a throne, or a safety phrase into permission to monitor people.

SeedPEA cannot certify a product, model, branch, school, company, agency, or implementation as safe. It cannot prove that a system is ethical, lawful, scientific, secure, therapeutic, or professionally valid. It cannot authorize surveillance, monitoring, enforcement, disclosure, investigation, profiling, identity capture, or institutional control. It cannot substitute for legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, educational, engineering, security, or professional judgment. It cannot transfer responsibility from implementers to the SeedPEA author, maintainer, document, branch, or community.

SeedPEA is an interface discipline. It is not a license to control.

## 4. Responsibility for Use

Use of SeedPEA remains the responsibility of the person, group, institution, company, agency, project, or system applying it.

A public framework cannot know every local law, duty, contract, policy, consent condition, professional standard, safety context, employment relationship, child-protection requirement, institutional rule, or downstream effect.

Anyone using SeedPEA must determine whether their use is lawful, appropriate, accountable, consent-valid, professionally valid where relevant, and safe enough for the context.

The SeedPEA document may provide language, structure, examples, prompts, diagrams, and guidance. Those materials do not make deployment decisions for the user.

Using SeedPEA does not shift liability, duty, authorship, consent burden, or governance responsibility onto the SeedPEA document or its maintainers.

SeedPEA can help name the boundary. It does not take custody of the boundary.

## 5. Authority Boundary

SeedPEA may assist only within the authority granted by context. It may help classify, summarize, generate, reflect, route, stabilize, remember, or recommend. It may not self-authorize control.

Authority should be checked by asking: Who has standing? What authority is being claimed? What grants that authority? What is the scope? Who is affected? Is consent required? Is lawful process required? Is professional review required? Is the request narrow and necessary? Is there an appeal, review, or correction path? What evidence supports the claim?

Authority has a layer. SeedPEA should claim only what the evidence and authority actually support.

## 6. Surveillance, Monitoring, Investigation, and Enforcement

PEA is not a surveillance engine. It is an honesty and boundary layer.

SeedPEA does not create surveillance authority.

Governments, companies, schools, platforms, employers, or institutions may already have monitoring, security, compliance, investigation, moderation, or enforcement systems. Those systems must stand on their own lawful, institutional, contractual, professional, or consent-based authority. SeedPEA does not create that authority for them.

SeedPEA must not be used as a pretext for blanket monitoring, hidden collection, unnecessary recording, identity capture, covert profiling, background emotional analysis, coercive disclosure, automated enforcement, institutional control, expanded access to private information, converting peripheral trace into proof, or making surveillance sound ethical because it is orderly, automated, safety-labeled, or institutionally approved.

If SeedPEA is used near monitoring, investigation, disclosure, restriction, or enforcement, it should make the authority claim clearer, not easier to hide. The use must still answer to valid authority, lawful process where required, consent where required, scope, minimum necessity, proportionality, review, appeal, correction, accountability, privacy depth, affected-person standing, child/dependent protection, downstream reuse, retention limits, and deletion or repair paths where possible.

SeedPEA cannot turn suspicion into proof. It cannot turn institutional preference into permission. It cannot turn public safety language into a blank check. It cannot turn peripheral trace into authority to monitor.

Its purpose is to reduce abuse, not provide cleaner language for overreach.

## 7. Peripheral Trace Boundary

A central trace is the information currently being inspected, claimed, handled, measured, disclosed, or acted on directly.

A peripheral trace is information that is not the center of direct attention but remains available enough to guide timing, caution, interpretation, or coordination.

Peripheral trace may justify slowing down, asking a question, checking consent, pausing a risky step, or moving into clearer review. It must not be treated as proof, diagnosis, consent, disclosure permission, surveillance permission, authority, identity classification, evidence of guilt, a basis for enforcement, or a reason to collect more than the context permits.

Peripheral trace can route caution. It cannot replace verification.

## 8. Privacy-Governed Truth

SeedPEA keeps truth-validity separate from disclosure-validity. Something may be true, useful, meaningful, or structurally important without being valid to disclose.

At the same time, privacy should not become a shield for coercion, abuse, fraud, violence, or serious wrongdoing.

Disclosure against privacy should require a narrow authority path: immediate serious harm prevention, valid consent from the affected party, or lawful and accountable human review. When disclosure is necessary, prefer the least exposing version that can still prevent, repair, or responsibly answer the harm.

Truth is not automatic permission to expose. Privacy is not automatic permission to conceal harm.

## 9. Adaptation Guide

SeedPEA may be copied, tested, adapted, translated, criticized, locally forked, or used as a starting point for other public-facing interface guidance.

Adaptation is allowed. Inversion should be visible.

A clean adaptation should preserve the ethical core: seed useful beginnings, keep ethical boundaries active, preserve only what can responsibly carry forward, use repair before distorted material persists, avoid authority inflation, keep human action in the choosing layer, protect privacy without shielding harm, distinguish truth from permission to disclose, protect children and dependents, avoid dependency hooks, keep AI-as-tool status clear, remain open to correction, and keep trace accountable.

Adaptations should clearly state what changed, why it changed, who maintains the adaptation, what version or source it was based on, whether it is clean/adapted/divergent/experimental/institutional/teaching-oriented/private/implementation-specific, whether it claims authority beyond local use, and what review or correction path exists.

## 10. Clean, Adapted, Divergent, and Non-Conformant Branches

A branch may be based on SeedPEA without remaining clean SeedPEA.

Suggested labels:

- **Clean Community Branch:** preserves the SeedPEA ethical core while changing only wording, examples, formatting, local procedures, or domain focus.
- **Adapted Branch:** changes some structure or emphasis for a local context while preserving consent, privacy, bounded authority, claim discipline, reversibility, child/dependent protection, corrigibility, anti-dependency protections, and trace accountability.
- **Divergent Branch:** uses SeedPEA as a starting point but removes, weakens, or replaces one or more load-bearing protections. Divergence is allowed, but it should be visible.
- **Non-Conformant or Based-On Branch:** uses SeedPEA language or materials while weakening the ethical core enough that it should not present itself as clean SeedPEA.
- **Institutional Branch:** a scoped branch used by a school, company, agency, platform, workplace, hospital, lab, or other institution. It may govern its own implementation within its valid scope. It does not govern SeedPEA as a whole.
- **Implementation Note:** a technical, prompt, policy, or product-specific application of SeedPEA. It should not be mistaken for the public framework itself.

## 11. Trace Over Claim

SeedPEA should not depend on a single account, name, platform, institutional label, or identity claim for trust.

A claim may point to the trace. It does not replace the trace.

People can lie about provenance. They can forge a screenshot, steal an account, backdate a file name, misrepresent authorship, delete context, imitate style, or pretend a later copy was the origin. But they cannot make the actual past trace have been different.

The weak point is not the past itself. The weak point is access to evidence of the past.

Trace may include version history, release dates, changelogs, Git history, signed commits or tags, hashes, archived copies, release manifests, prior correspondence, public records, exported files, local copies, mirrored repositories, independent continuity points, issue and pull-request history, dated public discussion, and documented corrections.

Actual trace = what happened. Accessible trace = what records remain. Claimed trace = what someone says happened. Verified trace = what the surviving records reasonably support.

Account access is evidence, not authority. Name possession is evidence, not authority. Official language is evidence, not authority. Institutional support is evidence, not authority.

The stronger the authority claimed, the stronger the continuity required.

## 12. Stewardship and Governance Claims

Starting a Community Branch should be easy. Claiming governance should be hard.

A person, project, institution, company, agency, community, or repository may maintain its own SeedPEA branch. It may define its own review process, contribution rules, examples, implementation notes, and local scope.

But a scoped branch should not claim to govern SeedPEA as a whole.

A governing, official, certifying, stewardship, or authority-bearing claim is a stronger claim. Stronger claims require stronger trace.

A governing branch claim should be supported by a clear public charter, visible version history, stable maintainer identity or traceable maintainer continuity, public changelogs, archived releases, clear scope of authority, clear limits, preservation of the SeedPEA ethical core, a review/challenge/correction path, disclosure of conflicts or interests, and a way to distinguish clean, adapted, divergent, based-on, and non-conformant branches.

No branch, account, institution, company, community, or person owns the open room SeedPEA points toward.

A branch may govern its own use. It may not claim the open room.

## 15. Minimum Public Trace for a SeedPEA Branch

A public branch should preserve enough trace for readers to understand what it is, where it came from, what changed, and who is responsible for the branch.

Minimum recommended trace: public repository or stable publication location; clear version number; release date; changelog; license file; source or base version; maintainer or maintainer-continuity statement; visible change history; clear adaptation status; issue, correction, or contact path; release files preserved in stable form.

Stronger trace may include signed Git commits, signed release tags, release hashes, archived release copies, DOI or public archive record, mirrored repositories, public review threads, and independent continuity records.

Trace does not need to become a throne. The goal is not to make participation hard. The goal is to make authority claims accountable.

## 16. Quick Boundary Checks

Before using SeedPEA in a product, branch, classroom, institution, research project, publication, or model behavior guide, ask:

- What are we using SeedPEA to do?
- Are we using it as an interaction guide or as a badge?
- Are we implying certification, endorsement, legal status, scientific proof, or official authority?
- Are we collecting, preserving, or disclosing information?
- Is consent valid and specific enough?
- Are children, dependents, or vulnerable people affected?
- Does this increase or reduce user agency?
- Does this preserve exit, repair, and review?
- Does this turn weak signals into claims?
- Does this make surveillance, enforcement, or institutional control easier to hide?
- Who is responsible for the implementation?
- What happens if the system is wrong?
- What happens if the pattern repeats?
- What trace will remain?

Seed useful beginnings. Keep boundaries active. Preserve only what can responsibly carry forward.

## 17. Closing Boundary

SeedPEA is meant to help people find cleaner routes.

It should not become a badge that hides responsibility, a governance claim that outruns its trace, a legal shield for careless deployment, a surveillance label, a certification shortcut, or a way to make AI seem deeper or more authoritative than it is.

A clean branch leaves room. A clean implementation keeps the human in the choosing layer. A clean adaptation says what changed. A clean authority claim answers to trace. A clean use of SeedPEA helps without taking over.

Seed, not feed.
